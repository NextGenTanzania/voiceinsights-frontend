Narrative Report — Access to Finance in Agriculture, Tanzania

A full written account of what we heard.

Terms of Reference. This study set out to understand how smallholder farmers across five Tanzanian regions — Morogoro, Iringa, Mbeya, Dodoma, and Arusha — experience access to agricultural finance, and what specifically stands between them and the credit they need to plant, expand, or recover from a difficult season. VoiceInsights Africa collected voice interviews from 100 farmers over phone and WhatsApp, achieving a 91% response rate.

What we found. Collateral requirements dominate the conversation. Sixty-one percent of respondents raised it unprompted, and it is the single most common reason farmers gave for being turned away from formal credit. As one farmer put it, the bank asked for a land title as collateral, but his land is not registered — so he could not get the loan. This is not an isolated story; it is the modal experience among farmers who tried and failed to access formal credit in this sample.

Interest rates and processing delays follow closely behind, cited by 54% and 42% of respondents respectively. Together with collateral, these three issues account for the large majority of negative sentiment recorded in the study — sentiment that, on closer inspection, is not directed at the idea of borrowing itself, but at the specific terms on offer today.

The mobile money paradox. Nearly nine in ten farmers (89%) already use mobile money, yet only 22% have accessed a formal bank loan. This gap is worth sitting with: farmers have adopted digital financial tools readily, but that adoption has not yet translated into meaningful credit access. Mobile money today functions mainly as a savings and transaction tool, not yet a bridge to larger agricultural financing.

What sets successful borrowers apart. The clearest pattern in the data is cooperative membership. Farmers who belong to a SACCOS are markedly more likely to report an active agricultural loan than those who do not — more so than any regional, age, or gender pattern we tested. One respondent described it plainly: since joining her SACCOS, she has been able to get a loan twice, for seeds and fertilizer, right when she needed it. This single relationship — cooperative membership to credit access — is arguably the most actionable finding in this study.

What this means going forward. In the near term, a simplified collateral pathway for smaller loan amounts, alongside more active promotion of existing mobile-money-linked micro-loan products, could unlock credit for farmers currently excluded by paperwork alone. Over the next six to twelve months, expanding SACCOS coverage in the two lowest-coverage districts — paired with financial literacy support bundled into the loan application process — addresses the deeper structural gap the data points to. Longer term, the collateral barrier is ultimately a land-title registration problem as much as it is a lending problem, and a simplified registration pathway for smallholders would address the root cause rather than its symptoms.

If nothing changes, the risk is not abstract: unmet credit demand among these farmers is likely to persist through the next planting season, with the farmers least able to absorb that cost — those without collateral or cooperative membership — bearing it disproportionately.
